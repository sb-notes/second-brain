## Family stepfather — haemorrhagic stroke (March 2026)

`stepfather-1` (Carciofi's stepfather, 68, retired, lifestyle factors included regular alcohol use and processed-food-heavy diet) suffered a haemorrhagic stroke at the airport while about to board a return flight to the UK after approximately four weeks of intensive travel through New Zealand and Australia. Sudden severe pain at top of head plus visual disturbance in one eye — classic thunderclap headache, hallmark of subarachnoid haemorrhage. Admitted to a major hospital in the destination city; hourly observation for 48 hours.

Diagnosis: bleed on the brain, most likely subarachnoid or intracerebral haemorrhage. Condition worsened by 22 March with additional neurological symptoms.

Surgery on 22 March 2026: 3-hour operation to remove blood clot (craniotomy or endoscopic evacuation). Confirmed successful around 9pm that day per `stepmother-1`'s message to Carciofi.

Neurological status post-operatively: loss of feeling on left side of body (left hemiplegia or hemiparesis), indicating right-hemisphere bleed. Speech and language likely preserved (left-hemisphere dominance). Cognition and personality likely more intact than would be the case with a left-sided bleed. Full recovery uncertain — medical team noted "only time will tell."

Likely contributing risk factors: probable undiagnosed or unmanaged hypertension, chronic moderate-to-heavy alcohol use, poor diet, four weeks of extreme physiological stress (jet lag, disrupted sleep, high stimulation, constant travel), acute situational stress at the airport.

Status: in specialist hospital care. Risk of re-bleeding elevated in first 14 days. Early physiotherapy expected to begin in hospital. Multidisciplinary team assessment for cognition, speech, swallowing, mobility. Fit-to-fly assessment minimum around 3 weeks subject to neurological clearance (altitude changes intracranial pressure risk). `stepmother-1` to contact travel insurance regarding medical repatriation. Rehabilitation trajectory: inpatient neuro-rehab then outpatient physiotherapy and occupational therapy; first 3–6 months are peak recovery window. Motorbike riding likely off-limits long-term — significant identity and wellbeing implication.

## Twin conjunctivitis (late February 2026)

Fox developed bacterial or viral conjunctivitis around 26 February — yellow dried mucus both eyes, red whites, watery eyes (pre-existing tendency). By Day 2: settling, weeping left eye only, right eye clearing. Treated with sterile saline plus cotton wool pads, wiping outer corner inward, every nappy change. Worked well.

Bunny caught it from Fox by 28 February — woke with right eye swollen and a third closed. Assessment: periorbital oedema secondary to conjunctivitis. Treatment: same saline and cotton wool routine plus warm compress (warm flannel, 2–3 minutes on closed eye) before each wipe — critical for swelling drainage. Apis Mellifica 30c added three times daily for the swollen eyelids.

Both resolved without escalation to chloramphenicol.

## Fox — cold sore (HSV-1, early March 2026)

Single vesicular lesion at lower lip margin, noted morning of 8 March 2026. Clinical presentation consistent with early-stage primary HSV-1 cold sore. Likely transmission via kiss from adult carrier during the extended family visit.

Treatment used: aciclovir 5% cream (OTC) 5x daily, dabbed gently with clean fingertip or cotton bud (not licensed under 12 but negligible systemic absorption). Coconut oil applied 1–2 hours after each aciclovir application as antiviral and soothing layer. Manuka honey UMF10+ dabbed before bed after final aciclovir dose. Avoid arginine-rich foods during active lesion (no raisins, chocolate, nuts); boost lysine foods (eggs, fish, chicken). Natrum Muriaticum 30c three times daily dissolved in teaspoon of water.

Infection control during active phase: no kissing near Fox's mouth, no shared cups, cutlery, face cloths, or dummies with Bunny. Bunny monitored for cross-infection — none occurred. Resolved within the expected 7–10 day timeline.

## Perianal abscess working diagnosis (29 March 2026, revised at A&E)

Initial presentation on Easter Sunday 29 March 2026 was assessed as perianal abscess: hard, hot lump under the skin to one side of the anus; constant background soreness with throbbing or stabbing pain every five minutes; pain worsened on sitting, walking, and during or after bowel movements; blood on tissue; mild dizziness; afebrile.

Working aetiology: directly linked to existing pelvic floor hypertonicity, repeated difficult bowel movements creating microtrauma from prior fissures, post-Slynde hormonal bowel motility disruption, and probable endometriosis-related pelvic inflammation.

Easter Monday progression — pain-free overnight, function restored to playdate by morning, then sudden severe deterioration mid-afternoon making walking impossible. Lump character changed from discrete mass to diffusely lumpy, hardened, reddish-purple area. NHS 111 called, GP callback advised attendance at `local-a-and-e-1`.

**Diagnosis revised at A&E (30 March 2026):** not a perianal abscess but an acutely inflamed anal fissure, possibly impinging on adjacent nerve. The pain-free overnight window was inconsistent with abscess (which would not give pain-free windows without drainage); the sudden deterioration on sustained walking represented friction and pressure irritating acutely inflamed fissure tissue; the red-purple discolouration and diffuse hardness were inflamed oedematous tissue, not abscess cavity. Anusol providing relief was consistent with fissure rather than abscess. Hepar Sulph dosing revised from 2-hourly (acute abscess dose) to 3x daily (sub-acute fissure dose).

Active fissure case continues in `dr-quinn-carciofi-gynae.md`.
