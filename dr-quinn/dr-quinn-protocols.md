# Dr Quinn — Family Protocols

Family-wide reference material that applies to more than one person or is used as a standardised template across the household. Person-specific clinical content lives in the per-person warm files.

## Love Me Feed Me protocol

Active from around 20 March 2026. This is the central feeding methodology for the children, designed to address food-scarcity anxiety inherited from the foster placement. The underlying clinical insight is that loud demanding at mealtimes is a signal of unmet food safety, not a behaviour problem. The children's nervous systems learned in foster care that desirable food (yoghurt, fruit, dessert items) was scarce and had to be secured before it disappeared. The protocol removes that scarcity signal entirely.

The mechanics are simple. At every meal, all components — main course, yoghurt, stewed apple — are placed on the table simultaneously before the children sit down. Nothing is sequenced or withheld. Children may eat in any order. Yoghurt is never used as a reward for eating the main course. The therapeutic logic is that the nervous system has to be able to *see* that everything is already available, repeatedly, over weeks, before the scarcity programme starts to relax.

Standard servings: half a dessert spoon of full-fat plain live-culture yoghurt at all three meals from around 29 March 2026 (originally lunch and dinner only). One heaped dessert spoon of stewed apple with chia seeds at lunch and dinner. Product specification: full-fat, plain, live-culture only — Yeo Valley Organic or Total Fage 5%. No low-fat, flavoured, or added-sugar varieties. Served at room temperature (Ayurvedic principle that cold dairy suppresses digestive fire, particularly relevant for Fox).

Marked reduction in food-demanding behaviour and mealtime anxiety in both children was confirmed within the first days of the protocol. This is documented as a significant early therapeutic win.

The next planned phase is full LMFM 'all you can eat of the good stuff' — unlimited access to carbohydrates and vegetables at each meal. Date of commencement to be recorded when it begins.

A meal-ending ritual is emerging — the "milk" transition phrase ("Oh Fox is going to have milk. Does Bunny want milk?") works as a meal-ending signal but is not yet consistent.

## Acute illness protocol (parents)

At first sign of cold or flu symptoms in Kiwifruit or Carciofi: elderberry syrup 1 tablespoon three to four times daily; zinc increased to 30mg daily; quercetin 500mg twice daily; vitamin C increased (extra camu camu doses in smoothie, two to three times through the day); 7 mushroom blend increased to 2 teaspoons daily. Continue 7–10 days, then return to baseline.

On-hand supplies: Sambucol or Higher Nature Elderberry Syrup; Quercetin 500mg (Natures Aid or Solgar).

One-time acute illness kit cost: elderberry syrup £7–10 plus quercetin £10–15, total £17–25.

## Children's feeding behaviour analysis (background)

Both children loudly request yoghurt at almost every meal, particularly after breakfast and lunch. Assessed as food-scarcity anxiety from foster care legacy rather than defiance. Fox's food anxiety is more pronounced and longer-documented. The implicit programme being acted out: "I need to secure desirable food now before it disappears." Loud demanding is the signal, not the problem — the underlying driver is unmet food safety and predictability. This analysis is what underpins the LMFM design above.

Health notes for the dairy component: Fox at 1 tablespoon per meal is well within safe range for his constipation protocol; do not allow portion to increase without monitoring stool output. Pause yoghurt during active mucus-heavy illness for both children — dairy thickens secretions. Bunny: monitor for subtle eczema flare (mouth or cheek dryness) in first two weeks of daily inclusion; very low risk given existing kefir tolerance.
