## Diagnostic picture (confirmed with high confidence, March 2026)

Primary diagnosis: vulvodynia and genitourinary syndrome — hormonally driven, secondary to Slynde (drospirenone-only progestogen-only pill, no oestrogen) combined with perimenopause onset.

Secondary: probable endometriosis (unconfirmed — laparoscopy was not performed in 2020 when polyps were found; remains unresolved).

Contributing: pelvic floor hypertonicity — never assessed by physiotherapy; strongly suspected given the clinical picture. This is the central unaddressed driver of multiple symptom strands and is the most important outstanding action.

## Symptom history

Onset November/December 2025. Generalised vulvovaginal soreness — not localised itch but inflamed-tissue soreness. Worse around period time, somewhat better between periods but never fully absent. Soreness or burning for hours or days after sex. Internal examination worsened symptoms (consistent with hypertonicity). Felt like a UTI despite clear urine tests.

All standard investigations clear: urine clear, vaginal swab clear, cervical smear normal (most recent a few months before onset), internal examination unremarkable. Thrush pessary tried and did not resolve. Antibiotics for cystitis tried and did not resolve. Standard medical approaches all failed because the cause is not infective.

## Perianal fissure case (active from late March 2026)

A perianal acute episode developed Easter Sunday 29 March 2026. Initial presentation was suggestive of perianal abscess: hard, hot lump under the skin to one side of the anus, pea-to-small-grape size; constant background soreness with waves of throbbing or stabbing pain every five minutes; pain worsened on sitting, walking, and during or after bowel movements; blood on tissue and occasionally on stool surface; mild dizziness assessed as pain-mediated vagal response; afebrile (36.4–36.6°C). Anusol applied with some relief.

Aetiology was clear from the outset: directly linked to existing pelvic floor hypertonicity causing abnormal perianal tissue pressure, repeated difficult bowel movements creating microtrauma from prior fissures, post-Slynde hormonal bowel motility disruption, and probable endometriosis-related pelvic inflammation creating a pro-inflammatory local environment.

The Easter Monday progression was telling. Pain-free overnight, then morning bowel movement with little pain and no bleeding, then a walk to a playdate functioning well, then sudden severe deterioration mid-afternoon making walking impossible. Lump character changed — no longer a discrete mass but a diffusely lumpy, hardened, reddish-purple area. NHS 111 called, GP callback advised attendance at `local-a-and-e-1` (no UTC walk-in available). Triaged to A&E.

**Diagnosis revised at A&E (30 March):** not a perianal abscess but an acutely inflamed anal fissure, possibly impinging on adjacent nerve — explaining the throbbing, radiating, wave-like pain pattern. The clinical reinterpretation: the pain-free overnight window was inconsistent with abscess (which would not give pain-free windows without drainage); the sudden deterioration on sustained walking represented friction and pressure irritating acutely inflamed fissure tissue; the red-purple discolouration and diffuse hardness were inflamed oedematous tissue, not abscess cavity; Anusol providing relief was consistent with fissure rather than abscess.

Prescription issued: GTN 0.4% (Rectogesic) topical cream, twice daily to anal margin. No surgical intervention required.

## Active fissure treatment protocol (from 30 March)

Prescribed GTN 0.4% applied to anal margin — initially twice daily. Anusol after each bowel movement and at bedtime. Warm sitz baths 10–15 minutes, two to three times daily and always post-bowel movement (warm water only, no soap). Paracetamol 1g as needed; ibuprofen 400mg for significant flares only. Ice for acute flares (10 minutes on, cloth barrier). Squatty Potty / footstool on toilet to reduce straining. Hepar Sulph 30c — one pillule three times daily for two weeks then reassess.

**Application correction (3 April):** GTN was being applied externally only rather than inserted to first knuckle as directed. GTN must reach the internal anal sphincter to be effective; corrected immediately.

**GTN side effects (4 April):** severe dizziness, pounding and spinning head, unable to sit or lie comfortably when upright. Mechanism: systemic nitrate vasodilation — cerebral vasodilation causing the headache, postural hypotension causing the dizziness. Not dangerous but debilitating; a well-documented reason patients stop GTN. **Protocol adjusted:** reduced to half-pea smear, once daily at bedtime only (not twice daily), applied at least 30 minutes after sitz bath, always lying down. If still intolerable at the reduced dose, request diltiazem 2% cream from GP as a direct substitute — same sphincter-relaxing mechanism, no nitrate headache side effect, equally effective.

Bowel management is central to fissure healing. Magnesium bisglycinate stepped up to half teaspoon at bedtime (therapeutic dose). Water 2+ litres daily. Olive oil 1 tablespoon daily in food. Flaxseed oil 1 teaspoon daily (smoothie or direct). Two kiwi fruit daily — evidence-based stool softener. Avoid white bread, white rice, excess dairy, processed food during the acute phase. Increase oats, cooked vegetables, pears.

Escalation path: review at 6–8 weeks. If fissure not healed despite cream and bowel management, the next step is colorectal surgery referral for Botox injection to internal anal sphincter — outpatient, high success rate. Pelvic floor physiotherapy remains the central long-term prevention intervention.

## Other associated symptoms

Grey vaginal discharge without odour appeared 31 March 2026. Most likely cause is Ovestin cream residue (oestriol cream is white and can appear grey when mixed with natural secretions and expelled the following day — common, benign, expected). Second possibility is bacterial vaginosis (classically grey or off-white, often with odour but odourless BV is common; low-oestrogen states are a known BV risk factor). Third possibility is normal perimenopausal discharge as the vaginal epithelium sheds while rebuilding under Ovestin. Action: note whether the discharge appears specifically after applying Ovestin (cream residue) or independently throughout the day (more likely flora-related). If it persists beyond 1–2 weeks or develops any odour, repeat vaginal swab via GP or Newson appointment. If BV confirmed: short course oral metronidazole plus probiotics (OptiBac For Women recommended).
